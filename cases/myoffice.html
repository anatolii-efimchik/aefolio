Smart office concept

Workspace management for 10,000 people with 7,000 seats — changing behavior through data, not restrictions.

Context

How it started

Imagine: a company built an expensive smart office. Sensors, IoT, smart meeting rooms, analytics. Everything shines and works. But buying enough desks — didn't happen. Or they didn't want to.

10,000 employees. 7,000 seats.

30% deficit — and this can't be solved by buying furniture.

When I started digging in, it turned out the task wasn't about 'making a booking system and fitting 10,000 people into one room'. The real question was different:

How do you turn an office with a seat deficit into a predictable and comfortable system — without bans and pressure?

It turned out yes — possible. But first I needed to understand what was actually happening.

Stakeholders

Who was in the game — and where was the tension

Before drawing a single screen, I figured out who is actually interested in this product — and what each of them wants.

This is where I understood: if you don't resolve the conflict of interests at the concept level — any technical solution will be unviable.

Stakeholder What they want The conflict
HR / Office management Occupancy control, filling rates Want mandatory booking and precise tracking
Employees Predictability, comfort, privacy Don't want surveillance and extra friction
IT / Security Integration with corporate systems Restrictions on data collection and storage
Team leads See which team members are in the office Want precise location of each person

The main conflict: HR wanted to see every employee on the office map in real time. Employees were categorically against surveillance.

My solution:

Show zone and headcount — '7 out of 12 from Marketing on floor 5' — but not each person's exact seat. Legally cleaner, and people are more willing to use the product.

Research

How I did it

I had no direct access to employees at a specific company, so I consciously built the research from two sources: industry benchmarks on hybrid offices, and in-depth interviews with relevant users. This let me verify not just individual opinions, but understand how far these patterns are confirmed at the market level.

Industry benchmarks

I studied 6 major industry reports: HubStar, CBRE, Microsoft Work Trend Index, Density.io, Kastle Systems, and HireHi.ru for the Russian market. In total — data from 300+ million sq. ft. of offices in 13 countries.

Based on this data I developed a guide for 10 in-depth interviews (30–45 min) with specialists from IT and product companies, age 25–40, hybrid schedule.

Synthesis

Only insights that appeared in 6+ profiles AND were confirmed by at least one benchmark made it to the final list. I tried not to rely on isolated observations or personal assumptions without data confirmation.

Modeling based on industry data is standard practice in corporate products when there's no direct access to the audience. More honest than 'talked to three friends', and more rigorous than gut feeling.

Insights

What I found — 5 insights

// тут вставить изображение · синтез инсайтов

Insight 1. People arrive early not because they want to — but because they're afraid

9 out of 10 profiles arrive 30–90 min earlier than necessary — just to grab a decent desk. This creates an artificial load peak at 9:00–10:00, even though actual occupancy in those hours is 20–30 pp below perceived (HubStar).

The problem isn't that there are too few seats — it's that nobody knows how many are actually free.

«I get in at 8:20, even though meetings start at 11. Afraid everything will be taken by 10»

Insight 2. Meeting rooms sit 'occupied' — with nobody inside

7 out of 10 profiles regularly arrive at a booked room to find it empty. According to Density.io, 25–30% of all bookings are ghost meetings.

This isn't a problem of inconsiderate people — it's a system without automation.

«I book for 15:00, come in — empty. Message them — silence, looking for another»

Insight 3. You go to the office to collaborate — but everyone's at home

6 out of 10 profiles came to the office specifically to work with colleagues — and found the whole team was remote. According to Microsoft WTI, 48% of employees name this as the main source of chaos in hybrid work.

The social value of the office collapses without one simple tool — team visibility.

«I come to chat, but everyone's at home. I sit alone and feel stupid»

Insight 4. Everyone goes to lunch at 1 PM — even though they could go at 2:30

10 out of 10 profiles complain about 10–15 minute queues at 1:00–1:30 PM. Meanwhile at 2:30 the canteen is nearly empty. The pattern is stable and predictable every day — but people only find out about it when they're already in the queue.

The problem isn't the size of the canteen — it's the lack of information in advance.

«At 1 PM there's a 15-min queue, at 2:30 it's empty, but I've already eaten»

Insight 5. Parking is a lottery, even though it could be predictable

6 out of 10 profiles with a car name parking as the main morning stress. Spots run out by 9:00–9:30. According to Kastle, on peak days load exceeds 90%.

This is solved by one tool — a load forecast before leaving home.

«Parking is a lottery. Was 15 min late»

HMW

How I reframed pain points into questions

  • How to help someone decide 'go in or not' — without fear and without an unnecessarily early arrival?

    → Load forecast, confidence before leaving home

  • How to eliminate ghost bookings so people trust the room status?

    → Live status from sensors, auto-cancellation of empty bookings

  • How to give team visibility — without violating each person's privacy?

    → Aggregate by zones, no tracking of specific individuals

  • How to help choose a lunch time without a queue?

    → Real-time data and gentle suggestions

  • How to make parking predictable?

    → Load forecast and alternatives in advance

Design

Design solutions

Core principle behind all decisions:

Data changes behavior better than rules. Not 'you can't come before 10 AM' — but 'current load is 18%, at 1 PM it'll be 98%'. The person draws their own conclusion.

// тут вставить изображение · сценарий «Утро сотрудника»

Solution 01

Load heat map

Before, people didn't know how loaded the office was — and played it safe by arriving early. I decided to give them this information right on the main screen.

Indicator Load What it means
🟢 Green < 40% Free — come comfortably
🟡 Yellow 40–70% Moderate
🟠 Orange 70–90% Busy — worth thinking about
🔴 Red > 90% Overcrowded
// тут вставить изображение · s11.png — тепловая карта загрузки

Non-coercive behavior change through information.

Hypothesis: if at least 15% of employees shift their visit by an hour, peak load could decrease by 8–10 pp.

Not taken: mandatory booking of a specific arrival time — coercion reduces adoption and causes resistance.

Solution 02

Three room statuses instead of two

Standard systems give two options: free or occupied. But there's a third scenario — the room is booked but nobody's inside. That's exactly what creates the problem.

Status Data source What happens
🟢 Free Calendar + sensor Come on in
🔴 Occupied Calendar + sensor Find another
🟡 Booked but empty Sensor (10 min no movement) 'Take it' button appears → booking auto-cancels in 15 min
// тут вставить изображение · s12.png — статусы переговорок

Why this way: people don't abuse ghost bookings intentionally — they just forget to cancel.

Hypothesis: automation reduces ghost bookings without penalties and without increased user resistance.

Not taken: penalty system for ghost bookings — the result would be the opposite: people would be afraid to book at all.

Solution 03

You see the team, but don't spy on people

People come to the office to work together — but don't know if the team will show up. I needed to give them this information without turning the product into a surveillance system.

  • 'In office 18, remote 3, on leave 2'
  • Floor and zone, but not the specific workstation
  • Each person decides whether to show their status (default: on)
// тут вставить изображение · s13.png — видимость команды

Why this way: team leads wanted to see the exact location of each person. But this is legally risky and reduces trust in the product.

Hypothesis: zone-level status answers the real question 'are colleagues coming?' without making employees feel surveilled.

Not taken: map with avatars on the floor plan — looks nice but privacy-conscious people will just turn off visibility, and the feature dies.

Metrics

How to measure success

North Star metric

% of employees who found a seat within ≤ 10 minutes of arriving — direct reflection of how predictable the office has become for a person.

Metric Now Goal in 6 months Source
Found seat in ≤ 10 min 58–62% 82–87% HubStar, CBRE
Ghost meeting room bookings 25–30% < 10% Density.io
Time to find a seat ~12 min ≤ 10 min Profiles
DAU — > 70% Forecast
Canteen load at peak 95–98% 80–85% Benchmarks
Office NPS — +20–30 pp Forecast

Guardrail metric: HR complaints about not finding a seat should not grow — otherwise we improve the experience for some at the expense of others.

First two weeks after launch

  • Day 7 Retention > 60%
  • Average daily opens > 2.5
  • % using the heat map > 40%

All figures are forecast benchmarks for a pilot, not launch results. Based on industry benchmarks, 10 interviews and my product assessment of expected effect. Actual values could only be confirmed after launch and real-audience measurement.

Principles

Principles that guided me

Not nice words — these are filters I checked every contested decision against.

1. Data instead of rules

If a person sees what's happening themselves — they make the right decision themselves. Bans and obligations are a last resort.

2. One platform, not a set of separate tools

Workstations, meeting rooms, canteen and parking — all in one solution. Fewer switches — higher engagement and use of each service.

3. Privacy is not a feature, it's a condition of trust

Without trust the product won't be used. Aggregated data, no precise tracking, users control their own visibility.

4. Better to warn than to help fix

A good system doesn't wait for a person to encounter a problem — it tells them about it in advance.
